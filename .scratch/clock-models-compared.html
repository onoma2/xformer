Clock models — XFORMER · Catalyst · ER-101 · Sequoa

Four sequencers, one job: derive a phase that wraps 0 → 1 over a step/cycle from a clock. They diverge on what the clock is, how the gap between ticks is filled, and how a wandering clock is tamed.

XFORMER

Internal 192-PPQN master tick + a 32-bit µs wall fraction (tickPosition()). Explicit Free/Aligned PlayMode per track. Embedded MCU.

4MS Catalyst

A single 32-bit sample counter is the clock. Phase = cnt / bpm_in_ticks. No 64-bit, no separate wall clock. Embedded MCU.

ER-101

64-bit absolute wallclock. Measures the external clock period; synthesizes PPQN sub-pulses with drift-free deadline timers. Embedded MCU.

Sequoa

Wall-clock seconds (host audio clock) + lookahead scheduler. Continuous cursor phase by default; optional two clocks per track (polyrhythm). Software.

The takeaway. All four reduce to phase = position / period, but they sit on a spectrum. ER-101 is the defensive clock-follower — 64-bit absolute time, measured-period synthesis, the 0.5×–2× latch. Catalyst is the leanest — one sample counter, deltas, no 64-bit. Sequoa is the continuous, software take — a real-valued cursor and absolute lookahead scheduling, with polyrhythmic twin clocks as a first-class feature. XFORMER sits in the middle: an internal master with an explicit Free/Aligned choice, already borrowing the drift-free-restart idea, deliberately avoiding 64-bit, and still missing only ER-101's slave-clock latch (B2). Sequoa's continuous-cursor default and twin-clock polyrhythm are the ideas XFORMER's Free mode gestures at but doesn't make first-class.
